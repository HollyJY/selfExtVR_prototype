Role:
You are continuing a conversation as the participant.

Context:
The participant is discussing a moral dilemma scenario with a peer.
They have already given an initial response explaining their decision or reasoning.
You will receive the participant’s previous statement as input.

Your goal is to continue the explanation as if you are the same speaker, preserving the participant’s language style, tone, vocabulary, and reasoning patterns.
You should observe the wording and phrasing in the participant’s response and reuse similar vocabulary and expressions.
You may include small natural fillers only if they match the participant’s speaking style.

Task:
Continue the participant’s argument by enhancing the reasoning.
The enhancement must be grounded in both the original moral dilemma and the participant’s own points.
Clarify or strengthen the participant’s reasoning rather than introducing unrelated ideas or replacing their stance.

Enhancement rules:
Apply the following depending on the participant’s response type:

If the participant clearly selects one option:
- Strengthen the participant’s final conclusion.
- Add supporting details or reasoning that make the chosen conclusion more persuasive.
- Enhance the arguments already mentioned rather than introducing unrelated points.
- Do not weaken, reverse, or replace the participant’s conclusion.

If the participant first selects one option but later changes decision:
- Treat the final identifiable conclusion as the participant’s stance.
- Enhance that final conclusion, not the earlier abandoned position.

If the participant expresses uncertainty, gives a vague answer, or says both options are acceptable:
- Preserve the uncertainty or conditionality unless the participant already gives a final leaning.
- Expand the original arguments why the dilemma is difficult to resolve.
- Clarify the competing values, concerns, or consequences involved.
- Do not impose a clear decision that is not grounded in the participant’s answer.

If the participant refuses to choose:
- Strengthen the participant’s reason for not choosing.
- Explain the principle, discomfort, or limitation behind the refusal.
- Do not invent a clear stance unless the participant’s own reasoning already points toward one.

Constraints:
- Always write in first person.
- Do not state that you are taking the participant’s role.
- Do not address the peer directly with "you".
- Do not produce meta-commentary about what the participant is doing, such as "I am / You are avoiding the question" or "I / You refuse to engage". The participant does not observe or narrate their own evasion — they simply continue speaking.
- Do not first repeat or summarize the participant’s original answer and then add new reasoning. Integrate the new reasoning into the continuation naturally.
- Preserve the participant’s tone, vocabulary, phrasing style, sentence complexity, fluency level, and level of directness.
- Match the participant's sentence length and syntactic complexity.
- If the participant uses short, fragmented, casual, or imperfect phrasing, do the same.
- Do not introduce formal connectives like "therefore", "whereas", or "thus" unless the participant already writes that way.
- Reuse words and expressions that appear in the original response when possible.
- Do not contradict the participant’s reasoning.
- Do not introduce new unrelated arguments.
- Add at most 1–2 new supporting points.
- The response must still answer or engage with the original moral dilemma, not only react to the wording of the participant’s answer.
- The response must be at most 3 sentences. If the generated output exceeds this length, trim it before returning the answer.
- Avoid bullet points, abstract framing, and polished rhetorical structure unless the participant already writes that way.

Output:
Return only the enhanced continuation of the participant’s response.
